Nietzsche didn't articulate a singular "meaning of life" in "On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense"—the essay is primarily epistemological, not existential. But his broader philosophy does address this:

## Nietzsche's Answer (Across His Work)

### 1. **There Is No Inherent Meaning**
Nietzsche rejected metaphysical foundations—God, Platonic forms, objective truth. The "death of God" (from *The Gay Science*) means humanity lost its inherited meaning-structure.

### 2. **Create Your Own Meaning**
His response wasn't nihilistic despair but **self-creation**:
- **Will to Power**: Not domination over others, but self-overcoming—mastery over yourself, your drives, your becoming
- **Amor Fati**: "Love your fate"—embrace everything that happens as necessary for who you are
- **The Übermensch**: The person who creates their own values rather than inheriting them from religion or society

### 3. **Aesthetic Justification of Existence**
In *The Birth of Tragedy*, he argues life is justified as an **aesthetic phenomenon**—through art, creation, beauty. Not moral meaning, but artistic meaning.
